Scriptural Heritage

The Craft & The Scriptures

🪔

Temple wood carving is not merely decorative; it is a spiritual science. Our workshop strictly adheres to the ancient Indian manuals of art, design, and architecture—the Shilpa Shastras.

Divine Proportions (Talamana)

According to the Shilpa Shastras, a deity sculpture must follow precise proportions (known as Talamana). A measurement unit, the Tala, is divided into 12 Angulas. By utilizing mathematical systems, our craftsmen calculate the exact relative height, width, and angle of every limb, ornament, and expression.

This strict alignment ensures that the finished carving is visually balanced and acts as a suitable focal point for meditation and spiritual focus. It is said that when these metrics are perfectly followed, the wood becomes a gateway for the divine presence.

The Sacred Timber

🪔

Selecting the wood is a sacred act. We use seasoned, sustainably sourced timbers, each carrying its own unique visual character, strength, and spiritual associations.

🍂

Burma Teak

Known as the 'King of Timbers', teak is highly prized for temple doors and pillars. Rich in natural oils, it resists moisture, pests, and weathering for centuries, developing a gorgeous deep golden brown color over time.

Primary Choice for Doors
🌹

Indian Rosewood

A heavy, dense wood with dark red-brown and purple grains. Its fine texture allows for extremely detailed carving, making it perfect for intricate relief panels of deities like Shiva Nataraja or Goddess Lakshmi.

Best for High-Detail Reliefs
🪔

Sacred Sandalwood

Highly aromatic and spiritually pure, sandalwood is historically used for small devotional statues and altar figures. Its smooth, close grain allows for miniature details, and its sweet fragrance lasts for decades.

Exquisite & Aromatic
Step-by-Step

The Journey of the Chisel

🪔

How a solid block of raw timber is transformed into a sacred masterpiece. Walk through the traditional phases of our carving process.

1
Step One

Tree Worship & Seasoning

Before any tree is harvested, a small prayer of gratitude is offered to its spirit. The logs are then sawn into planks and seasoned naturally in a dry shade for 2 to 3 years to prevent cracking or warping in the future.

2
Step Two

Sacred Geometry Sketching

The artist draws a grid on the wood using charcoal, mapping the dimensions in strict compliance with the Talamana guidelines. Key anchor coordinates are established to ensure perfect symmetry and proportion.

3
Step Three

Rough Chipping (Bahu)

Using heavy mallets and large iron gouges, the craftsman removes excess wood around the sketched outline. This shapes the block, bringing out the basic relief levels of the composition.

4
Step Four

Fine Chiselling & Ornaments

Using tiny precision chisels (some custom-made for specific curves), the craftsman details the ornaments, crowns, fingers, and facial features. This phase requires intense concentration and steady hands.

5
Step Five

Finishing & Natural Polish

The completed carving is sanded down using fine abrasive leaves, then rubbed with natural sesame oil and beeswax. This preserves the timber, highlights the natural wood grains, and gives it a warm, divine glow.
